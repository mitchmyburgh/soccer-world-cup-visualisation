Literature Review

The first visualisation, "The Clubs That Connect The World Cup"[1], from the New York Times shows the connections between players, the clubs they play for and the teams they represent in the soccer world cup. A screen shot is shown in Figure 1.

The visualistion allows trhe user to see these connections by hovering over a particulat 'bubble' to see the connections to and from it. The visualisation makes good use of greyscale, fading unnecessary data on hover while still giving the viewer the ability to get an overall feel for the entire dataset while focusing on a specific piece of data. This is shown in Figure 2

"The Clubs That Connect The World Cup" makes use of circles, whose size repsesent the number of players connected to them, however showing one dimensional data as a two dimensional shape is problematic as it is difficult to compare the size of circles, especially when they are similar in size. Furthermore the visualisation breaks this convention by making large circles for popular players (see Figure 2) in order to display their images. The visualisation also uses green on white which has a low luminance contrast, making it harderto see than a darker colour.

The second visualisation, "A Look at the Most Interesting World Cup Visualizations and Champion Predictions"[2], seen in Figure 3, shows the probability of different teams winning the soccer world cup.

The visualisation also makes good use of greyscale, highlighting the most likely winner, while still allowing the viewer to have an overview of the data. The data is also easily visible and comparable, and multiple dimensions are shown.

The visualisation uses yellow on white which provides a low luminance contrast making it difficult to discern, and coupled with this it is not immediately apparent what the data means.
